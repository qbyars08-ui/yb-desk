# Research Brief: NVDA  

## What it is  
NVIDIA designs GPUs for gaming, data‑center AI, professional visualization and automotive. The company also sells AI software platforms and chips for inference and training. Revenue is split roughly 50% gaming, 45% data center, the rest automotive and OEM.  

## The setup  
The stock has rallied ~150% over the past 12 months. Earnings have beaten expectations for three quarters in a row. Guidance for FY25 shows revenue growth of 20‑25% and a higher‑margin AI services segment. The balance sheet is strong: cash > $10 bn, net debt negligible. Valuation is elevated: forward P/E ~ 55×, EV/EBITDA ~ 30×. The run has been driven by macro AI hype, strong demand for H100 GPUs, and a bullish narrative around generative AI.  

## Bull case  
- AI adoption accelerates faster than consensus. Enterprises double down on GPU clusters for inference.  
- Data‑center share expands to > 55% of revenue by FY26. Higher‑margin services and software boost operating income.  
- New product cycles (e.g., Hopper‑2) deliver performance gains that keep NVIDIA ahead of rivals.  
- Strategic partnerships with hyperscalers lock in long‑term contracts.  
- Cash flow remains robust, enabling share buybacks and R&D investment without diluting shareholders.  

## Bear case and kill vectors  
- AI demand plateaus. Competitors (AMD, Intel, custom ASICs) close the performance gap, eroding pricing power.  
- Supply chain constraints limit GPU shipments, leading to missed revenue targets.  
- Regulatory scrutiny on AI chips or export restrictions to China cut a sizable market.  
- Macro slowdown reduces corporate capex, hitting data‑center orders.  
- Valuation remains detached from fundamentals. A single earnings miss could trigger a sharp correction.  

## Desk verdict  
The data shows a strong growth narrative but also a very high valuation. The run is not definitively over; price action will likely stay elevated if AI spend continues to beat expectations and new product launches meet roadmap targets. A slowdown in AI spending, a successful competitor launch, or a regulatory shock would change the picture quickly. The current risk/reward balance leans toward caution until the next earnings cycle provides clearer guidance on sustainable demand and margin trajectory.
